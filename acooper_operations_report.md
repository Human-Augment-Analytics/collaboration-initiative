# Project Manager Reflection - Charleston Train Group

## Define what reoccurring tasks you are doing within your project group?

I am primarily focused on task tracking. The Charleston train group is still in the exploratory phase, which means that structure and deliverables are not super clear yet. Instead, the lack of structure and the ambiguity of the tasks means that tracking progress towards the (ambiguous) goals is not well defined. This makes it harder for researchers to know how the overall project is doing on a scope longer than one to two weeks. My focus on task tracking is aimed at remedying this.

For that, I have been encouraging researchers to submit their weekly reports, and memorializing what they report. Their reports are not always super clear, as tasks can subtly change over the course of just one week (due to the exploratory nature of the project.) I have attempted to publicize the tracked tasks over time, but this has been a work in progress.

## How are you assessing your role as the project manager and its effect on the overall project?

When I joined the project, researchers were not submitting any weekly reports, and told me when I interviewed them that they didn't have a clear long term view of the progress and successes of the group. There has been a marked change in this — now researchers consistently submit their weekly reports. In addition, tasks have become more concrete, actionable and it has become easier to know if they are completed.

## What challenges & success have you observed in your group?

The primary challenge is the ad-hoc working style of the group. In conversation with other managers involved with Prof. Mussman projects, this seems to be true for many of his projects. Therefore, trying to add some order via management has been made a bit more difficult. Additionally, much of the important conversations and task delegation occurs in meetings, interspersed with technical details and conversation that is hard for a manager to follow. This makes it challenging to extract actionable items and track them effectively without asking researchers for clarification after the fact.

However, the successes are notable. The shift toward systematic weekly reporting represents a significant cultural change in the group's approach to project transparency. Researchers have demonstrated increased investment in HAAG processes by now regularly contributing reports without prompting. Moreover, when task tracking was first introduced, researchers expressed very positive feelings about the initiative, recognizing its value in bringing clarity to their work.

## How are you collaborating with stakeholders to ensure your role is supporting and motivating the group?

Given the context of the early stage research of this group, I have focused on being supportive in management duties. I'm working to enable researchers to see the bigger picture and stay motivated through task tracking, rather than imposing rigid structures that might conflict with the exploratory nature of the work.

I believe that this has served as motivating process for researchers, to take a few minutes each week to reflect on their own progress, which was usually pretty sporadic and changed over the span of the week. However I did not receive much explicit feedback from the researchers about this.

## What feedback have you received on your role solicited or unsolicited?

The feedback I've received has been encouraging. Most notably, researchers now regularly contribute their weekly reports without prompting, which demonstrates increased investment in the HAAG processes and suggests they see value in the reporting infrastructure. When task tracking was first introduced, researchers expressed very positive feelings about the initiative, appreciating the clarity it brought to their work.

However, the feedback has remained primarily formal and transactional. While researchers engage with the task tracking system, we haven't yet moved into deeper conversations about how the management approach is affecting their day-to-day work, morale, or sense of progress. This suggests there's room to foster more open dialogue about the impact of these processes.

## What plans do you have for enhancing your role as the project manager with the remaining time in the semester?

With the semester ending in late April, I have approximately three weeks to consolidate and enhance the management infrastructure I've built. My plans include:

1. Align researchers with HAAG processes: I plan to share the tracking documents with the researchers again, to remind them that their weekly reports were crucial in the HAAG administration of their research efforts.

2. Ensure continuity: I will write up my experiences with this research group, and clean up  the tracking documents to aid future managers with their administration of this research group.

The goal is to leave the group with not just better tracking practices, but both internal and external (ie. future managers) processes that help deliver research goals as this project matures.
